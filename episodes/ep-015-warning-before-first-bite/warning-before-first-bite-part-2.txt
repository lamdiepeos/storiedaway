THE MARRIAGE REGISTERED TWICE

The ambulance took Everett to a hospital his regular physician did not control.

Dana rode with him. A child-protection detective and a social worker stayed with Milo in a private family room, gave him clean clothes and breakfast, and contacted the neighbor listed in his school file. No one treated him as evidence to be passed from adult to adult.

Everett's independent tests showed traces of the same cardiac-active compound in his blood. A specialist explained that the result could help account for months of faintness, tremors, and irregular heartbeat, but only a full medical review could determine duration and harm. Everett remembered every meal Camille had carried to his office and every appointment Dr. Keel had insisted she attend.

The folded document from Camille's handbag was more immediate. It was a draft incapacity petition. If two named physicians declared Everett unable to manage his affairs, temporary voting control of Halcyon Mobility would pass to his spouse and chief operating officer, Philip Crane.

One physician was Rowan Keel.

The other had died six months earlier.

At noon, the laboratory confirmed that the substance had been applied to Everett's serving after it left the kitchen. Police detained Camille for questioning but did not announce a charge before the chain of evidence was complete.

Milo asked to speak with Everett once the social worker agreed it would not burden him. He brought the brass key.

His mother, Elena Torres, had worked for three years in Halcyon's executive records office. She was meticulous, poorly paid, and nearly invisible to the people whose signatures she processed. Three weeks earlier, she discovered that one of Everett's electronic signatures had been attached to the incapacity petition before the document was finalized.

She traced the file to Philip's private archive. There she found medical invoices, surveillance photographs of Everett, and a scanned marriage certificate from Nevada dated eleven years earlier.

The names on it were Camille Voss and Philip Crane.

Camille had married Everett seven years ago under her maiden identity without disclosing that her first marriage remained legally active. Her courtship, the charitable gala where they met, and even the broken elevator that gave them an hour alone had been arranged through Philip's staff.

“Mom said the love story was a schedule,” Milo said.

Elena copied what she could and stored it in a rented locker. Before she could meet Everett, Dr. Keel signed an emergency psychiatric hold claiming she was delusional and dangerous. Two men took her from her apartment. Milo saw the charcoal-suited one remove her phone.

He stayed overnight with their elderly neighbor, Mrs. Alvarez. The next morning, men claiming to be county workers arrived without identification and asked for Elena's files. Mrs. Alvarez sent Milo out the rear stairwell with the key and called police. Milo followed the emergency instructions his mother had taught him: take the bus to Maison Lark on Sunday, when Everett always ate breakfast at nine.

“Why didn't she come to my security office?” Everett asked.

Dana answered before Milo had to. “Because Philip approved half the hires in my department. I am auditing every officer now.”

The brass key opened locker 214 at a monitored storage facility. Detectives obtained a warrant before entering. Inside were printouts, an encrypted drive, a voice recorder, and a sealed envelope addressed to Everett. Elena had separated originals from copies and left a written chain of where each record came from.

The final recording captured Camille and Philip arguing.

Camille wanted to stop. Philip reminded her that the petition would be filed after Sunday's breakfast, before independent doctors could examine Everett. He also threatened to expose her first marriage and make her solely responsible for everything already done.

Everett listened twice.

Coercion might explain Camille's fear. It did not erase the plate, the lies, or the years she had continued.

Dana entered with a location update. Elena had been found alive at a private behavioral clinic outside the city, registered under a misspelled surname. A judge had ordered her release to an independent hospital while investigators examined the fraudulent hold.

Milo's whole face changed. “Can I see her?”

“As soon as her doctor and your social worker say it is safe,” Dana promised.

Everett looked again at the Nevada certificate. The woman who had slept beside him for seven years was another man's wife. The executive he called his oldest friend had designed their first meeting. The doctor who explained his worsening health had helped create it.

Then Everett's company counsel called.

Philip Crane had convened an emergency board meeting for four o'clock.

On the agenda was Everett's immediate removal for incapacity.
