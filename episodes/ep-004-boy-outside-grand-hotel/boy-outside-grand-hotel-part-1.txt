Six-year-old Ethan broke away from his mother beneath the chandeliers of the Grand Aurelia Hotel and offered his sandwich to a homeless boy no one else would look at.

The boy raised his face—and the laughter, camera flashes, and conversation around them died at once, because he looked exactly like Ethan.

Then he lifted a faded hospital bracelet and asked Ethan's mother, "Then why did you leave me?"

Three minutes earlier, the hotel entrance had been a theater of polished wealth. Black cars rolled beneath the gold-lit canopy. Photographers called to arriving actors and business leaders. Inside the glass doors, crystal chandeliers threw warm light across marble floors.

Ethan Ward walked beside his mother, Caroline, wearing the navy blazer she had buttoned twice in the car. She was due upstairs at a charity dinner organized by the Ward Foundation. In one hand, Ethan carried a paper lunch bag because he had refused the hotel food on their last visit and Caroline had learned not to argue with a hungry six-year-old.

Then Ethan heard a cough near the outer wall.

A boy sat beyond the velvet ropes where the chandelier light faded into the cold blue evening. He looked about Ethan's age. A torn gray hooded jacket hung from his narrow shoulders. His bare ankles showed above worn shoes, and both hands were tucked beneath his arms for warmth.

"Ethan!" Caroline called as her son pulled free.

He ran past the doormen, past a row of cameras, and dropped to one knee in front of the boy.

Ethan opened the bag. "Here. You can have my sandwich."

The boy stared at the sandwich before slowly looking up.

A woman near the ropes gasped. Someone else whispered, "They look like twins!"

The resemblance was not vague. The boys had the same dark eyes, the same straight black hair, the same small dimple beside the left corner of the mouth. Even their startled expressions mirrored each other.

Phones rose across the entrance.

"Ethan, wait!" Caroline pushed through the gathering crowd. Irritation vanished from her face when she saw the second child.

She stopped so abruptly that a photographer collided with her shoulder.

The boy flinched and pulled the sandwich to his chest. A strip of cracked white plastic slipped from beneath his sleeve. Caroline saw faded blue lettering, a date, and the beginning of a surname.

WARD, INFANT B.

Her handbag struck the marble.

"They told me only one baby survived." Her voice barely carried over the crowd.

Ethan looked from his mother to the boy. "Mom... why does he look like me?"

The other child held up the bracelet as though it were evidence in a trial. His lower lip trembled, but his eyes did not leave Caroline's.

"Then why did you leave me?"

Caroline sank to her knees. "I didn't. I swear to you, I didn't."

The hotel entrance erupted. Guests shouted questions. Camera shutters hammered. One man tried to step closer, and the homeless boy scrambled backward until his shoulders hit the wall.

Ethan moved between him and the crowd.

"Stop filming him," Ethan shouted. "He's scared."

That simple sentence restored Caroline's ability to move. She stood, took off her evening coat, and held it out without approaching the boy.

"What is your name?"

"Noah."

"Noah, I'm Caroline. I gave birth to two boys at Saint Gabriel Hospital six years ago. They told me the second baby died before I could hold him. I buried an empty sealed casket because the doctors said I should not see what had happened."

Noah watched her with the suspicion of a child who had learned that adult explanations often arrived before adult disappearances.

"A man named Ray said you paid him to take me," he said.

Caroline went still. "Who is Ray?"

"He had my papers. He said rich people throw away what they don't want."

The hotel security director reached them and began forcing the crowd back. Caroline asked him to call the police and an ambulance, then sat on the cold pavement several feet from Noah.

She did not touch him. She did not call him her son. She only placed her coat and Ethan's unopened bottle of water between them.

"You don't have to believe me tonight," she said. "But you will not have to sleep outside tonight. The police can stay with us. A doctor can examine you. You can choose where I stand."

Noah looked at Ethan.

Ethan tore his sandwich in half. "I'll eat with you. Then you know it isn't bad."

For the first time, Noah's grip loosened.

The ambulance arrived without sirens. As paramedics guided Noah toward it, he allowed Ethan to walk beside him. Caroline followed at the distance Noah had chosen.

Behind them, the hotel doors reflected two identical small figures moving through the same pool of golden light.
