A mud-covered boy burst through a funeral in the rain and shouted, "Stop the burial!"

The grieving father seized him beside the white coffin, but the boy pointed at the lilies trembling on its lid.

"She's alive." He pointed toward the coffin. "Your wife poisoned her."

Twelve-year-old Leo Santos had run nearly two miles from the funeral home. His wet gray shirt clung to his ribs. Mud covered his knees, and blood from a cut palm mixed with the rain.

The mourners turned as he broke between black umbrellas. Security moved to intercept him, but Daniel Harrow reached him first.

"Please, sir, don't bury your daughter!" Leo cried.

Daniel gripped his shoulders. Grief had hollowed his face during the three days since fourteen-year-old Lily was declared dead.

"What are you saying?"

"She's not dead. She's only in a coma!"

The crowd gasped. Daniel's hands tightened.

"Don't say that."

Leo pointed toward the coffin. "I heard her breathe. I saw her move."

Beneath a black umbrella, Daniel's wife Vanessa became completely still.

Leo turned toward her. "Your wife gave her something to drink."

Daniel recoiled. "My wife would never hurt her."

But his voice broke.

Leo had delivered flowers to the Harrow house on the night Lily collapsed. Through the open kitchen door, he saw Vanessa stir clear liquid from a medicine vial into a glass of lemonade. Lily drank it and fell minutes later. Vanessa told the arriving paramedics that her stepdaughter had a rare seizure disorder.

The following night, Leo sheltered from the storm behind the funeral home after missing the last bus to the youth residence. He heard a faint scrape inside the preparation room. Through a low window, he saw Lily's finger move beneath the white cloth. When he tried the door, the funeral director chased him away.

He returned at dawn. The coffin was already being loaded for burial.

"Sir, look me in the eye," Leo pleaded. "She's still alive."

Daniel turned toward the coffin.

Vanessa caught his sleeve. "This child wants money. Lily is gone. Don't let him desecrate her funeral."

Leo slammed his palm against the lid. A lily slid to the ground.

From inside came one soft knock.

The sound was barely louder than the rain, but Daniel heard it.

"Open the coffin."

The funeral director refused, citing the sealed medical release. Daniel tore away the floral straps himself. Two security guards helped lift the lid.

Lily lay pale beneath the satin, her breaths so shallow that the fabric near her throat moved only once every several seconds. Her right index finger twitched.

Daniel screamed for an ambulance.

Vanessa stepped backward. Detective Amara Chen, attending as a family friend, noticed and took hold of her arm.

"You are staying here."

Paramedics found a weak pulse. They ventilated Lily on the cemetery path and rushed her to the regional trauma center. Leo rode in a police car behind them, still clutching the empty medicine vial he had taken from the Harrow trash before running to the funeral home.

At the hospital, toxicology identified a veterinary paralytic combined with a sedative. The mixture suppressed movement and respiration while leaving intermittent awareness. A competent neurological examination would have found brain activity.

Lily had not received one.

The death certificate bore the signature of Dr. Martin Krell, Vanessa's former business partner.

Doctors administered supportive treatment and an antidote to the sedative component. Shortly after midnight, Lily opened her eyes.

Daniel stood beyond the glass because police and doctors needed to speak with her before family contact. Lily blinked once for yes when Detective Chen asked whether Vanessa had given her the drink.

Then she whispered two words through the oxygen mask.

"Leo knew."
