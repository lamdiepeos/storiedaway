THE TABLE SET FOR TWO

Cyrus Wren pleaded guilty after the recovered ledgers matched bank records and handwriting experts tied him to the false placement summaries. The court imposed a prison sentence, ordered restitution from seized property, and appointed an independent administrator to locate every person affected by his files. His daughter's company returned funds it could not lawfully justify; investigators found no proof that she had known their original source, and the judgment said so plainly.

Blake pleaded guilty to assault and criminal damage. The sentence included probation, community service supervised outside Bellweather, and a prohibition against contacting Elias or Nora. The outcome did not transform him into a better man. It simply established a boundary and a record.

Elias's corrected identity documents arrived in a plain envelope. For the first time since childhood, his birth certificate, medical file, and government identification carried one continuous name: Elias Rowan. The insurance restitution went into an account controlled by him alone. He used part of it for a small apartment, dental treatment, and training in archival restoration. Julian offered him a house; Elias said no. Julian learned that accepting no was part of being a brother.

Their reunion unfolded without a montage. Some days they spoke for hours. Other days Elias ignored Julian's calls because being found did not erase the years of not being found. Julian sometimes apologized for things he had not caused, and Elias sometimes punished him for a life he had not chosen. A family counselor taught them to name the difference between guilt and grief.

They visited their mother's grave in early autumn. Her marker read Anna Rowan, beloved mother, but only Julian's adopted surname appeared in the cemetery's next-of-kin file. Together they submitted the correction. Elias placed the restored carousel photograph beneath the flowers only long enough to take a private picture, then returned it to its archival sleeve.

"She kept looking," Julian said.

Mara had found Anna's petitions in three county offices, each redirected to Cyrus. She had died believing both sons might still be alive.

"Then we put that in the record too," Elias answered.

They did. The revised archive attached Anna's petitions to both brothers' files and removed the false death notices without erasing evidence that they had existed.

Bellweather funded a statewide Sibling Search Desk, but Julian could not control it. Its board was led by adults who had experienced foster separation; its finances were public; its services were free. Elias took a paid technical position restoring damaged letters and photographs. He refused to become the campaign's inspirational mascot. His work mattered more than his face.

Six months after the plate shattered, Bellweather Café closed for one morning. Not for a press event, but for repairs to the floor tile cracked beneath Elias's meal. Nora suggested preserving one small porcelain fragment in a shadow box near the staff room, not the dining room. Beneath it, a card read: Dignity is not a reward for good fortune.

When the doors reopened, Elias arrived in a clean gray jacket of his own choosing. Customers recognized him, but the staff had been instructed not to stare, photograph, or offer anything he had not requested.

Julian waited at the same table. Before him sat two intact plates and the joined carousel photograph. He had left the chair opposite him empty.

Elias stopped beside it. "Is this where you offer me half the company?"

"No," Julian said. "This is where I offer lunch. You can leave whenever you want."

Elias considered him, then sat.

Nora served the meal and set the bill beside Julian. Elias moved it to the center.

"Brothers split things," he said.

Julian smiled, but his eyes filled. "Some things."

They ate slowly. They argued about whether their mother had sung the storm song too fast. They compared the scars beneath their chins and discovered that each had spent years inventing a different explanation for the same childhood fall. Nothing restored the missing decades. Nothing made hunger, deception, or silence harmless.

But when the brass bell chimed and rain began against the windows, neither man mistook the sound for an ending.

Outside, the city continued without knowing that two erased boys had become visible in the same room. Inside, Elias reached across the table—not for money, proof, or rescue, but for his half of the photograph.

Julian gave it to him.

This time, they each kept one half by choice, and they knew exactly where to find the other.
