Daniel ripped his hand out of his mother's grip in the middle of the crowded avenue.

"Mom... that boy has my face."

Victoria followed his shaking finger and saw a barefoot child beneath a lamppost wearing Daniel's eyes, Daniel's mouth, and the same tiny dimple beside his lip.

For one impossible second, the entire street went silent around her.

Car horns still blared. A delivery truck still rattled past. A woman in heels brushed Victoria's shoulder and muttered something irritated. But none of it reached her. All she could see was the boy on the pavement, thin knees pressed to his chest, a stale piece of bread in one dirty hand, staring back at her oldest nightmare.

He could not be there.

Eleven years earlier, Victoria Harrington had given birth to twin boys.

Only one had been placed in her arms.

They told her the other had died before she woke.

She had spent every birthday since then wearing half of a bronze twin locket beneath her clothes, a private wound she never let anyone touch.

Now the missing half was hanging from the neck of a starving child who looked exactly like her son.

Daniel took one cautious step forward. His navy blazer was still neat from school, his shoes polished, his hair combed into the same careful part Victoria had fixed with her own hands that morning. Across from him, the barefoot boy's coat was ripped at one shoulder, his jeans too short, his cheeks hollow from more hunger than any child should know.

Yet their faces were mirrors.

"Why does he look like me?" Daniel whispered.

Victoria opened her mouth, but her throat locked shut.

The boy on the pavement rose slowly, as if sudden movement might scare them away. He could not have been more than eleven. He held himself with that wary stillness children learn when the world has taught them that every stranger might be danger.

Then his gaze dropped to the chain around Victoria's neck.

His whole expression changed.

He touched the tarnished bronze locket hanging against his own chest and took one trembling step toward her.

"Lady," he said, voice thin with cold and nerves, "why are you wearing my necklace?"
