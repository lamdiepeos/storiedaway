Victoria's knees almost gave out.

Without thinking, she pulled her own chain into the light. The metal caught the pale afternoon sun. Daniel frowned and leaned closer. He had seen his mother kiss that locket every year on his birthday, but she had never once explained why.

The boy held his piece out with both hands.

"The woman who raised me said if I ever saw the other half, I had to show it."

Daniel looked from one locket to the other. "Mom... what is happening?"

The two halves were not identical by accident. They were a pair, broken from one whole. Daniel reached toward the boy as though pulled by something deeper than curiosity. His fingers shook when he fit the edges together.

Click.

The lock engaged with a soft metallic snap that landed harder than a scream.

Daniel stared down.

Inside the opened locket were two tiny infant photographs sealed under scratched glass. Two newborns wrapped in hospital blankets. Two bracelets around two tiny wrists.

One read DANIEL.

The other read SAMUEL.

Victoria made a sound so raw that several pedestrians turned.

"No," she breathed, though the proof was already in her hands. "No, no, no..."

The barefoot boy flinched, as if he had heard rejection before and expected it again.

"My name is Sam," he said quietly.

The world tilted.

Victoria dropped to her knees right there on the sidewalk in front of him. She stared at his face, the curve of his brow, the shape of his lashes, the little scar near his chin that could only have come after birth, after a lifetime she had not been there to protect.

"Sam," she whispered, and the name shattered her.

Daniel stood frozen beside her, staring at the boy and then at his mother. "Are you saying... he's my brother?"

Sam looked frightened by the question, frightened by the woman crying in front of him, frightened by the expensive people beginning to slow down and stare.

"I don't know everything," he said. "I only know what she told me before she died."

He reached into the lining of his coat and pulled out a folded envelope, creased and dirt-stained from being opened too many times and then hidden again.

"Mrs. Alvarez raised me," he said. "She got sick last year. Before she died, she told me this letter belonged to my real mother. She said there were dangerous people who didn't want me found, so I had to wait until I saw the necklace."

Victoria recognized the handwriting before she even opened it.

Nurse Elaine Mercer.

The same nurse who had carried Daniel to her recovery room with tears in her eyes and a voice that had sounded too gentle to question.

Victoria's fingers trembled as she unfolded the paper.

Mrs. Harrington,

If this letter has reached you, then my cowardice has finally run out of time. Your second son was born alive. He did not die. He was taken from the hospital nursery under orders paid for by your mother-in-law, Eleanor Harrington. She told me the family name would not survive scandal, weakness, and division. I told myself I was saving my own children by taking her money. I have regretted it every day since.

The boy was placed through a private arrangement with a woman named Rosa Alvarez after your file was altered. I kept one half of the locket with him because I could not bear to bury the truth completely. Forgive me if you can. If not, I deserve that too.

Victoria stopped reading because she could no longer see through the tears.

Daniel grabbed the paper from her hand and scanned the lines with a child's growing horror.

"Grandmother did this?" he asked, voice cracking.

Sam stepped back as if he had already become too much trouble simply by existing.

"I didn't come for money," he blurted. "Mrs. Alvarez said I should find the truth, not ask for anything. I can leave if you want. I just needed to know if I was crazy."

Victoria surged forward and caught his face gently between her hands before he could retreat another inch.

"Do not ever say that again," she said, tears spilling freely now. "You are not leaving. You are not crazy. And you are not alone."

Sam stared at her, stunned.

Daniel looked at him with a mixture of awe, grief, and the strange instant loyalty children sometimes find faster than adults.

"If you're Sam," he said slowly, "then you're my twin."

Sam swallowed. "I think so."

Daniel held out his hand.

Sam looked at it for one frightened second, then took it.

That was when a sleek black car glided to the curb beside them.
