THE BODY AGAINST THE BLACK SUV

The first sound was Caleb Rowan's body striking the hood of his father's federal SUV.

His backpack slipped from one shoulder and burst open across the wet parking-garage floor. A sweatshirt, two schoolbooks, an inhaler case, and a black leather badge wallet scattered beneath the fluorescent lights.

"Hey!"

Officer Owen Briggs forced the sixteen-year-old's arm behind his back. Caleb's cheek pressed against cold metal while travelers near the terminal elevators slowed and raised their phones.

"That's my dad's car!"

Briggs tightened his grip. "Your dad doesn't drive federal plates."

Caleb had returned to the garage because his father, Daniel, had left the inhaler case in Caleb's backpack during the security rush. Daniel had remotely unlocked the assigned vehicle so Caleb could collect it before their flight. The confirmation still glowed on Caleb's phone beside the rear tire.

Briggs never looked at it.

He reached into the spilled bag and lifted the leather wallet. Daniel had placed it there by mistake when emptying his pockets at the checkpoint.

"Now fake federal ID too?" Briggs said.

Caleb's fear sharpened. "Don't open that!"

He forced himself not to pull against the officer's grip. Daniel had taught him that sudden movement could be misread, but every calm sentence seemed to make Briggs angrier.

"Check the phone," Caleb said. "The unlock confirmation is on the screen. Call the agency number on the plate record. Ask for a supervisor."

"You don't give orders here."

"I'm asking you to verify it."

A woman beside the elevator called out that she had seen the SUV lights flash before Caleb opened the door. Briggs told her to stand back. She did, but kept recording. Another traveler moved Caleb's inhaler case away from a shallow stream of rainwater and left it visible on the concrete.

The officer flipped it open anyway.

Tires sounded at the garage entrance. Two black agency vehicles stopped behind the patrol car, controlled rather than crashing, and a small protective team stepped out. Daniel Rowan crossed the concrete in a dark navy coat, his expression held tight enough to frighten the officer more than shouting would have.

"Take your hands off my son."

Briggs froze. Caleb lifted his head. "Dad..."

Daniel did not touch the officer or issue a threat. He identified himself, told Briggs to preserve the scene, and asked the arriving airport supervisor to summon medical assistance. Only then did Briggs release Caleb.

The boy's wrist was already red and swelling. Daniel picked up the badge wallet, then crouched beside Caleb.

"Can you move your fingers?"

Caleb nodded, breathing too fast.

Daniel's eyes moved past him to the SUV's open rear door. The hard-sided evidence case that had been secured on the back seat was gone. Only a rectangular impression remained on the upholstery.

His face changed.

"Where's the case?"

Caleb went pale. "I thought you took it."

Every agent turned toward the empty seat, but Daniel raised one hand before anyone ran. "Lock the exits lawfully. Preserve every camera. Nobody chases a guess."

Across the garage, elevator doors were closing.

A traveler pointed. "Someone pushed a luggage cart in there."

The doors sealed before the supervisor could stop them.

Briggs looked toward the elevator and then away too quickly. Caleb saw it.

"He came after the rear door opened," Caleb said. "There was another man here first. Gray maintenance jacket. He took something from the car. I yelled, and then this officer grabbed me."

Briggs shook his head. "The kid was breaking into a government vehicle."

"The access log will show who unlocked it," Daniel replied. "And the cameras will show everything else."

Paramedic Lena Ortiz examined Caleb beside the SUV. She placed his wrist in a temporary splint and recommended hospital imaging. Daniel wanted to go with him, but Inspector Ava Chen, who led the evidence review, pulled him aside.

"Your son is the victim and a witness," she said. "You cannot direct this investigation."

Daniel looked at Caleb, then at the missing-case imprint. "Agreed. You have it."

He surrendered his work phone, vehicle access records, and authority over the scene. Ava separated every witness, collected the travelers' original recordings, and ordered Briggs's body-camera unit secured before anyone could alter it.

The elevator returned empty.

On its wet floor lay a torn strip from a gray maintenance jacket and one brass luggage tag. Caleb recognized the tag. It had been attached to the missing case.

Briggs stopped calling the arrest a misunderstanding.

He began asking whether he was free to leave.
